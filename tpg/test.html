Bloomberg (Dec 15, 2010) - Top Glove Profit Slides on Latex prices, weaker dollar.
By Barry Porter - Dec 15, 2010

Top Glove Corp., the world’s biggest rubber-glove maker, said its profit dropped for a second successive quarter in the three months ended Nov. 30 on higher latex costs and a weaker U.S. dollar.

Net income fell 45 percent to 36.1 million ringgit ($11.5 million) in its fiscal first quarter, the Malaysian company said in an exchange filing today. Average latex prices were 57 percent higher than a year ago, while the dollar was 9.3 percent weaker against the ringgit, said Top Glove, which sells its products in the U.S. currency.

“Top Glove will continue to share some of these higher costs with its customers, even though there will be a time lag for the high cost to be passed down to them,” it said.

The Selangor-based company’s shares surged 188 percent last year as global demand for medical rubber gloves outstripped supply amid a wave of global flu scares. Manufacturers, including Top Glove, responded by adding production lines as international H1N1 flu outbreaks began to ebb.

Its shares fell 1.8 percent to 5.40 ringgit at 3:52 p.m. in Kuala Lumpur today. The company released its earnings during the midday trading break.

Sales climbed 4 percent during the quarter to 491.5 million ringgit. Demand has been “normalizing with the excess capacity situation,” the statement said.

The company reported a 21 percent drop in profit to 45.1 million ringgit in its fiscal fourth quarter ended Aug. 31.

Business Times (Dec 7, 2010) - MRB: Rubber sector to stay buoyant.

By Ooi Tee Ching
bt@nstp.com.my
2010/12/07

The natural rubber (NR) industry, after a rally this year, can expect another buoyant year in 2011, says the Malaysian Rubber Board (MRB).

Tight global supply, declining stocks and seemingly unstoppable growth of China and India point towards a buoyant 2011 for the industry, MRB said.

The price of SMR (Standard Malaysian Rubber) 20 was at a new historic high of RM13.35 per kg on November 11 this year.

China and India will continue to be the main focus for the NR market as their automotive industries are poised to register double-digit growth, at least in the next two years, MRB said in a statement yesterday.

The board said the rally in 2010 had been bullish due principally to tight supplies, speculative rubber futures, impact of Thailand's imposition of a new cess effective October 1 and dwindling stocks in major producing and consuming countries.

"In recent months, output has been affected by abnormal rainfall. Local prices are also boosted by the strengthening of the ringgit."

MRB said worldwide stock levels were at all-time low and Malaysia's rubber stockpile was equivalent to five weeks of world demand, compared to 10 weeks in the past.

"Contributing factors include abnormally heavy rainfall and flood affecting producing countries."

MRB said the seasonal lull of wintering from January to March is set to reduce the country's output further by up to 40 per cent.

Rubber planting and tapping are seen a useful strategy to alleviate poverty among rural folks and bridge the income level gap with urban dwellers.

As much as 95 per cent of land planted with rubber trees in Malaysia is tapped by smallholders.

MRB said almost half of smallholders in Malaysia are solely dependent on rubber tapping for their income.

The board noted that higher rubber prices in the international markets had resulted in smallholders securing monthly income of RM2,000 to RM3,000.

High rubber prices, since 2006, also have prompted hijackings of latex-filled tankers and theft of cup lumps.

MRB acknowledged that thefts are more rampant in times of high rubber prices.

The regulator has warned the industry not to take in latex that is suspected to be hijacked or stolen.

It said it will suspend or cancel licences issued if persons caught were found to be guilty.

It is common knowledge that rising rubber prices tempt suppliers to default on lower-priced contracts.

MRB has also warned rubber exporters not to defer shipment or cancel contracts.

Business Time (Wednesday June 17, 2010) - Top Glove Q3 profit jumps 53pc.

Published: 2010/06/17

Top Glove Corp Bhd (7113), the world's biggest rubber glove maker, continued its strong run as third quarter net profit jumped 53 per cent thanks to resilient demand.

Malaysia's rubber glove producers have been enjoying good profits since influenza A (H1N1) was declared a pandemic last year.

" ... the group is confident of continuous growth and good profitable performance in this financial year ended August 31 2010," it said in a statement to Bursa Malaysia yesterday.

Top Glove's net profit for the quarter to May 31 2010 was RM64.48 million against RM42.2 million. Revenue jumped 49 per cent to RM555.9 million.

The rubber glove maker said it is experiencing strong profit growth momentum as demand for gloves continues to increase, especially from the emerging markets.
In the first nine months to May 2010, its net profit jumped 78 per cent to RM200.22 million from a year ago. Revenue for the nine months rose 39 per cent to RM1.54 billion from RM1.11 billion a year ago.

Its balance sheet strengthened further with a net cash position of RM273 million and free cash flow of RM147.2 million for the period ended May 31 2010. It also fully redeemed some RM35 million bonds before its maturity in December 2010.

During the quarter, 1.37 million treasury shares were sold in the open market for RM17.28 million. Subsequent to May 31, a total of 5.25 million treasury shares were disposed for RM64.49 million.

Top Glove has done well despite latex prices spiking 24 per cent and the US dollar weakening by 3 per cent.

This year, it expects to return 40 per cent of its profits to investors.

Top Glove currently has 20 factories, 371 production lines with production capacity of 33 billion pieces of gloves per year and 10,900 employees. It has more than 900 customers worldwide and exports to more than 180 countries.

The Star (Saturday January 9, 2010) - Smart Glove deserves due recognition

OUR law firm represented Smart Glove Corp Sdn Bhd and Henry Schein in Tillotson nitrile glove litigation at the US International Trade Commission (ITC), as well as in the subsequent appeal to the Court of Appeals for the Federal of Circuit (CAFC).

We wish to provide several clarifications on the article entitled Tillotson case shows united stand by glove makers on page B2 of StarBiz on Jan 5, 2010.

It is correct that quite a number of “Respondents” participated in the ITC’s investigation, challenging the Tillotson’s allegation of patent infringement.

But by the time of the appeal to the CAFC, the only parties defending the ITC’s favourable decision were Smart Glove, Henry Schein and the ITC’s General Counsel.

The US distributors Ansell and Cardinal, which had worked actively at the ITC, settled. And the remaining Respondents simply did not participate in the appeal.

Certainly, Smart Glove and its partners, which shouldered the burden at the appeal, are entitled to congratulations for their success against the Tillotson patent.

Had Tillotson won the case, the ITC could have excluded the accused gloves from entry into the US and forbid the sale of gloves already in the US.

I would like to add that the CAFC decided the case in record time – just two business days after the oral argument.

This unusual speed suggests the strength of the case presented by Smart Glove and Henry Schein.

Westerman, Hattori, Daniels & Adrian, LLP.

Business Time (Monday January 4, 2010) - Glovemakers want guarantee on gas supply
Malaysia's rubber glove makers have appealed to the government to allocate more natural gas to them as they are fast becoming a significant contributor to the country's exports

Malaysia's rubber glove makers are willing to pay the market price for natural gas, over a period of time, but the government needs to guarantee that they will have enough supply.

"We are willing to pay market price, on a phase-by-phase approach," Malaysian Rubber Glove Manufacturers' Association president Lee Kim Meow said.

"We appeal to the government to allocate more natural gas to our sector as we're fast becoming a significant contributor to the country's exports," he told Business Times in an interview in Petaling Jaya, Selangor.

Manufacturers like glovemakers and ceramic producers use gas as their main fuel source.



Currently, Petroliam Nasional Bhd (Petronas) has fixed the annual supply of gas to industries in Peninsular Malaysia and the bulk, or about 63 per cent, goes to the power plants.

The manufacturing sector uses another 11.6 per cent of natural gas.

"Many of our members are unable to expand like before because there is uncertainty in supply of natural gas and foreign labour," Lee said.

At present, companies using less than two million standard cu ft per day (mmscfd) pay RM24.54 per million metric british thermal unit (mmbtu) of gas.

Those using more than two mmscfd pay RM32.56 per mmbtu.

The rubber industry has been the pillar of Malaysia's economy since colonial times, and continues to contribute to export earnings and employment.

Spurred by the government's industrialisation drive in the 1980s, Malaysia is now the world's top exporter of rubber gloves, catheters and latex threads.

Malaysian-made gloves account for two-thirds of the global market of 180 countries. In 2008, rubber glove makers raked in RM7 billion in export earnings, and last year's performance is likely to be the same.

The last time the government raised electricity and natural gas rates was on July 1 2008.

Commercial and industrial users faced a 26 per cent price increase, while smaller businesses like sundry shops, shopowners, small restaurants and cottage industries saw an 18 per cent increase.

The increases were unavoidable because of record petroleum prices that year. Crude oil hit a peak of US$147 (RM503) a barrel in July, and the price of gas followed suit.

In August 2008, fuel prices in the international market started to fall. By the last quarter of 2008, prices had plunged by more than 75 per cent to US$35 (RM120) a barrel.

Following public appeal, the government reduced electricity tariffs by 2.54 per cent from March last year. Offices and factories' electricity bills were reduced by 2.7 per cent and 5 per cent respectively.

Its “strong profit growth” was the result of “cost saving measures implemented at all factories, improvements in product quality, productivity, as well as aggressive market strategies,” the company said in the statement today.

To reduce their reliance on natural gas, all but Latexx and Kossan are now using biomass facilities.

The main issue for investors is the possibility of a glut given the industry’s aggressive expansion. We do not think this is a problem given the strong demand and good long-term prospects in developing countries where per capita consumption is low.

On top that, MARGMA and the rubber glove companies believe that prospects for the rubber glove industry will continue to improve given the continuous support by the government and the favourable outlook for the demand for rubber gloves. — CIMB Research, Dec 9

The Star (Saturday, 5 Dec 2009) -China's rise as a world power


Most Malaysian CEOs are positive and consider it inevitable.

IS China’s status as a rising world power a bad thing? For those who are not necessarily comfortable with the United States hegemony in the global economy, the answer is no, of course. Top Glove Corp Bhd chairman Tan Sri Lim Wee-Chai is one of them.

“China serves as a long awaited commercial balance. The current US downturn is a case in point, in which China is leading South-East Asia into growth,” he says.

He points out that the world now has China as a huge alternative market to do business with. The resulting trade will generate a lot of spin-off, he adds. The country’s new-found prosperity offers opportunities for foreign investors.

At the same time, the world economy gains from investments and consumption by the Chinese.

Green Packet Bhd chief executive officer Puan Chuan Cheong adopts a similar standpoint.

He argues: “China’s rise is positive. It had supported the world’s economic growth during last year’s financial crisis. It is a substantial contributor to the world GDP (gross domestic product), has the biggest output, and presents a massive consumer market that few can ignore.”

Plantation consultant Mahbob Abdullah also sees all-round benefits in China’s economic dynamism. “I think it’s very good for everybody that China has become a global economic force. The palm oil industry, for example, has gained because China’s rising demand for the commodity has opened up the market.

“On the other side, the Chinese population’s nutrient intake improves. Their annual per capita consumption of oils and fats has gone up to 20kg, and there’s still room to go higher.”

These points probably resonate less with people who persistently question China’s track record on human rights and the impact of the country’s progress on the environment. However, this criticism has not quite diverted Beijing from its growth strategy.

In fact, Puan is certainly not alone in believing that China will be the biggest economy in the world in time to come.

To some extent, China’s climb to the top of the economic heap draws some comparison with Japan’s journey to developed nation status, thus breaking the dominance of the Western world.

Japan too relied on manufacturing goods for the export markets to drive its economic development, gradually moving up the value chain. Like China today, Japan also exported its expertise in building infrastructure to poorer countries, and this was often linked to development aid.

Many of Japan’s consumer goods companies started out by churning out copycat products with inferior quality but eventually matured into international brands such as Sony, Toyota and Nintendo. There are signs that this may happen in China as well.

But there are crucial differences between Japan and China. For one thing, China’s vast population dwarfs that of Japan. Says Puan, “Comparing the population size of China and Japan alone, the former’s economic growth will be substantially greater than that of Japan decades ago.”

Top Glove’s Lim points out that China is unlikely to experience Japan’s flat growth over the past decade because he believes that China’s population and domestic consumption will ensure sustainable growth.

“Another big difference is that Japanese companies manufacture a lot of their products overseas. China’s goods are mostly produced in the country and are then shipped overseas,” he adds.

Nevertheless, there is also the concern that China’s economic structure is not entirely sound. Says a businessman, who has been involved in a concession-based business in China for several years, “In the China economic model, efficiency is not always a key objective.

“The Chinese are all for public-private partnerships. The government is more than willing to help entrepreneurs with things like generous tax breaks and land allocation. The yuan is also kept low to help exporters.”

In Poorly Made In China, his book on his experiences representing importers in South China, Paul Midler wrote: “Chinese manufacturers had other reasons for being in business, and they operated in a world where economic principles differed.

“One aspect that made manufacturing different in China was the symbiotic relationship formed between the export manufacturing sector and government.”

The problem with this scenario is that the cosy relationship between the state and state-owned businesses often results in poor financial and operational discipline.

For now, it appears that the drive, initiative and strategic thinking of the Chinese are sufficient to keep the economy pumping.

“The Chinese are very aggressive. When something needs to be done, it will get done. For example, they will work hard to initiate contact with a potential investor,” says Datuk Lim Hock San, managing director of LBS Bina Group Bhd, a property developer with a presence in Zhuhai in China.

“If not directly, they will do so through others, and they will be persistent. They will find a way.” LBS is a property developer that has a presence in Zhuhai in China.

While some see the aggressiveness as part of the can-do attitude, others are less appreciative. An executive director of a listed company that ventured into China in 2003 says after the foreign investors come in, they sometimes find that the rules have been changed to protect the local players.

Says a senior executive of another listed company, “You often hear about people being cheated in China ventures and about businesses cutting corners because of the get-rich-quick mentality.

“But we found that working with the China GLCs (government-linked companies) is a different experience. The Chinese are not fearful of God; they are fearful of their government.”

Top Glove to extend price hikes

 

By Ooi Tee Ching

Published: 2009/10/15

Top Glove Corp Bhd (7113)will continue to raise glove prices, as latex becomes costlier and the US dollar weakens, to maintain profits.

It last increased the prices in July.

The company recently posted its best annual net profit since 2001 for fiscal 2009 and doubled dividends. It is also optimistic of keeping its profit margin at more than 10 per cent in the year ending August 2010.

As the world's biggest glovemaker capable of churning out 31.5 billion pieces and commanding 22 per cent of the global market share, Top Glove's price move is keenly matched by its rivals.

"Latex prices had started to climb again since July and the US dollar had weakened since September. The combination of these factors have led to higher glove prices," said chairman and managing director Tan Sri Lim Wee Chai.
"We need to protect our shareholders' interest," he told stock analysts and journalists at a briefing in Kuala Lumpur yesterday.

Raising glove prices is an opportune move as currently global demand outweighs supply. Since the World Health Organization (WHO) declared influenza A (H1N1) a pandemic four months ago, rubber glovemakers have received more orders, especially from the US and Latin American countries.

According to WHO, the flu has infected more than 375,000 people throughout the world and claimed more than 4,500 lives since it was first detected in Mexico and the US in late March.

While some of the 40-odd glovemakers in Malaysia are able to ramp up capacity to meet the sudden demand, most are already running at full capacity around the clock.

"We continue to receive additional orders from Brazil, Argentina and Mexico," said executive director Lee Kim Meow.

In previous years, when supply was more than demand, glovemakers undercut each other by giving discounts. Between 2006 and 2008, Top Glove made a gross profit margin of less than 10 per cent.

Top Glove's dividend policy is to pay out 30 per cent of profits.

"Based on the measures we've taken to protect profit margin, we should be able to keep to our dividend policy in the current financial year," said Lim.

Business Time: Top Glove payout soars on record profit

By PAULINE NG IN KUALA LUMPUR (Business Times)

MALAYSIA'S largest glove maker, Top Glove Corporation, plans to reward its shareholders with a bumper dividend payout of 22 sen per share or double the previous year's, following record earnings on the back of surging demand for rubber gloves.

H1N1 driven: Top Glove plans to boost its 19 factories by opening two more next year, with 16 production lines targeted for completion in Q1 and 16 more by July

Seen as a natural proxy for the heightened global demand for rubber gloves after the outbreak of the H1N1 virus this year, shares of Top Glove have doubled this year, yesterday rising another 18 sen or 2.25 per cent to RM8.15, versus the benchmark increase of one per cent.

In line with the industry, Top Glove's earnings have been rising steadily as fear of the H1N1 contagion drove orders. For the last quarter ended August, Top Glove's profit jumped 121 per cent to RM55 million (S$22.5 million) on revenue of RM427 million.

Full-year earnings improved 56 per cent to RM168 million while revenue increased 11 per cent to RM1.53 billion. Earnings per share came to 57.34 sen, up from 37.18 sen previously.

Top Glove's board has recommended a special dividend of six sen a share in addition to the final dividend of nine sen per share, in view of the 'good performance and stronger cash flow position' of RM197 million in cash and free cash flow of RM260 million. An interim dividend of seven sen a share had been paid earlier.

The proposed payout was the highest since the company was listed in 2001 and executive chairman Lim Wee-Chai was confident of continued growth and 'good profitable performance' in the current fiscal year.

Malaysian companies have barely been able to keep up with the surge in demand. Top Glove's plant utilisation came to 90 per cent in the last quarter while others claim near full capacity.

Where others are cutting back, glove makers are ramping up, albeit cautiously. Top Glove's 19 factories and their 355 production lines already churn out 31.5 billion pieces annually, but the company plans to open two new factories next year, with 16 production lines targeted for completion in the first quarter of next year and another 16 lines by July.

Even cost increases - owing to higher latex prices - have been successfully absorbed by buyers; with Top Glove having raised its average selling price by 7-11 per cent. Its profit after tax margin for FY09 rose by some 11 per cent - only in FY01 has the margin been better at 11.4 per cent.

Analysts say that its defensive earnings support higher valuations, and Hwang-DBS has raised its target valuation to RM8.50 based on 14 times price earnings.

Mr Lim, who controls 35 per cent of the company he founded, appears to have taken shareholder feedback to heart. It was at a media and analyst briefing last year that he had solicited views, and found a preference for fatter dividends and more bonus shares.

Small by global standards, the glove player's market capitalisation now stands at almost RM2.5 billion. Its free float is some 51 per cent.
